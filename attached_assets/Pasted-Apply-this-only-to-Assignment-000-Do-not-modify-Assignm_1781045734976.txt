## ASSIGNMENT 000 STRUCTURE

Revise Assignment 000 into these clearly labeled SOC workflow stages.

Each stage should have:

* stage name
* current purpose
* investigative question
* tool or action used
* what the player learned
* what remains unknown

Keep all explanations concise.

---

## STAGE 1 — TRIAGE

Purpose:
Decide whether the alert deserves investigation.

Player mindset:
“What looks unusual?”

Scenario:
Sensors flagged repeated traffic to WS-4471 from an outside address.

Player actions:

* inspect available files
* read network snapshot
* identify repeated external activity

Tools:

* ls
* cat
* grep

Important:
These are Linux-style triage commands.

Explain subtly:
These commands help analysts inspect artifacts and search evidence.

Learning:

* IP addresses represent systems
* internal vs external matters
* repeated activity deserves review

Do not immediately declare it malicious.

Use uncertainty:
“This may be normal automation, scanning, or something suspicious. Investigate before deciding.”

---

## STAGE 2 — SOC TOOL ANALYSIS

Purpose:
Use analyst tools to validate whether the activity is expected or suspicious.

Player mindset:
“What evidence supports or weakens suspicion?”

Tools:

* whois
* ports
* compare baseline

Clarify:
These are SOC analyst tools, not basic Linux commands.

Learning:

* whois helps identify ownership
* port analysis shows which services were contacted
* baseline comparison checks whether behavior matches known-good activity

Important:
Add at least one legitimate or benign traffic example so the player learns comparison.

Example:

* 198.51.100.20 = CDN, known-good
* 10.0.5.1 = internal DNS, known-good
* 203.0.113.77 = unknown external source

This teaches:
not all external traffic is bad.

---

## STAGE 3 — CORRELATION

Purpose:
Connect multiple clues into one analyst conclusion.

Player mindset:
“How do these clues fit together?”

Evidence to correlate:

* external source repeated
* touched multiple unrelated ports
* unknown or unregistered ownership
* not on baseline

Also show benign contrast:

* CDN is on baseline
* DNS is internal
* approved systems have expected behavior

Learning:
One clue is not enough.
Analysts build confidence from multiple clues.

Add a short correlation summary:

Example:
“Multiple unrelated ports alone is not enough. Unknown ownership alone is not enough. Off-baseline alone is not enough. Together, they form a pattern worth escalating.”

---

## STAGE 4 — ESCALATION DECISION

Purpose:
Decide whether the case should be escalated.

Player mindset:
“Do I have enough confidence to escalate?”

Use a simple decision prompt.

Do not make it feel like a school quiz.

Frame it as:
Analyst Escalation Decision

Example:
Based on your evidence, what should happen next?

A. Escalate as suspicious reconnaissance activity
B. Close as normal CDN traffic
C. Ignore because no malware was found
D. Block all external traffic immediately

Correct logic:
Escalate as suspicious reconnaissance activity.

Explain:
The right decision is not based on one clue. It is based on repeated source activity, unrelated ports, unknown ownership, and baseline mismatch.

---

## STAGE 5 — RESPONSE / CONSEQUENCE

Purpose:
Show that analyst decisions affect operations.

For Assignment 000, keep consequence simple and low-risk.

Do not require real containment yet.

Show what happens after escalation:

Example:
CyberCorp SOC queue updated.
WS-4471 flagged for monitoring.
Source 203.0.113.77 added to watchlist.
No production systems were blocked during orientation.

This teaches:
early analysts often escalate and monitor rather than immediately block.

Important:
Do not teach beginners that every suspicious event means “block now.”

That is unrealistic.

---

## STAGE 6 — SOC DEBRIEF

Purpose:
Convert the tutorial into retained learning.

Debrief should include:

What happened:
An outside address repeatedly contacted WS-4471.

How the analyst investigated:
Reviewed evidence, checked ownership, analyzed ports, compared baseline.

Why it mattered:
The activity matched reconnaissance indicators.

What was not done:
The analyst did not immediately block everything.

Real analyst takeaway:
SOC analysts do not guess. They build confidence from multiple clues and escalate when the pattern is strong enough.

Concepts learned:

* triage
* Linux command basics
* SOC tools
* internal vs external
* ports
* baseline
* correlation
* escalation
* consequence
