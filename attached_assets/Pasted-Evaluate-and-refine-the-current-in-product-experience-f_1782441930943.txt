Mission name:
Protect Sensitive Information

Scope:
Focus only on the in-product experience. Do not focus on workshop facilitation, instructor scripts, or broader workshop-goal framing outside the product.

Current concerns:
Mission 1 still risks feeling too much like a quiz instead of a cybersecurity career simulation. Important evidence can blend into terminal output, the investigation flow may not feel enough like real analyst work, and the end of the mission may still need a stronger debrief and progression moment.

Goal:
Refine Mission 1 so it feels like a realistic, beginner-friendly cybersecurity career simulation rather than a static quiz. The player should feel like a Junior Security Analyst investigating a possible sensitive-data exposure, reviewing evidence, making a judgment call, and receiving a professional mission debrief at the end.

Core design principles:
1. Make the player feel like they are doing the job, not answering a test.
2. The mission should require investigation, judgment, and communication.
3. Important evidence should be visually distinct from terminal chatter.
4. The mission should be readable, immersive, and easy for a beginner to follow.
5. The mission should end with a satisfying debrief that reinforces learning and gives clear next actions.
6. The tone should feel like a Blue Team / security operations career simulation, not a classroom quiz.

Player role:
The player is a Junior Security Analyst.

Mission scenario:
An employee exported a file containing customer information and shared it with overly broad permissions. The player must investigate whether the file contains sensitive information, whether it was shared improperly, whether anyone unauthorized accessed it, how serious the issue is, and what should happen next.

Important design requirement:
Make each step of the 5-step mission structure feel distinct, purposeful, and clearly tied to the player’s goal. The flow should not feel like a long chain of generic activities.

For each step:
- clearly communicate what the player is doing now
- explain why this step matters to the investigation
- show what output or progress the player will produce by completing it
- make the transition to the next step feel earned and logical

Avoid presenting the mission as a flat checklist of tasks. Instead, present it as a sequence of meaningful investigation phases with clear stage titles, purpose statements, and completion signals.

Use the following structure as player-facing phases:
1. New Case Assigned
2. Evidence Collected
3. Investigation in Progress
4. Assessment Required
5. File Incident Report

Each phase should have:
- a clear title
- a one-sentence purpose
- a visible output or deliverable
- a completion state that reinforces progress
