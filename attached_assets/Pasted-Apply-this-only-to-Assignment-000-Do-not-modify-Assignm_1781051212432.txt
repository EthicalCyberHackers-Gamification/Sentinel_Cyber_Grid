## SOC WORKFLOW STAGE BAR

Create a visual step-by-step workflow bar:

1. TRIAGE
   Question: What looks unusual?

2. SOC TOOL ANALYSIS
   Question: Does evidence support suspicion?

3. CORRELATION
   Question: How do the clues connect?

4. ESCALATION
   Question: Do I have enough confidence?

5. RESPONSE
   Question: What operational action changes?

6. DEBRIEF
   Question: What did we learn?

The active stage should be highlighted.
Completed stages should remain visible but subdued.
Future stages should be locked/subdued until reached.

This teaches that SOC analysis follows a process.

Do not reveal all devices and connections immediately.

Reveal devices, connections, and zones as the player advances.

Stage 2 — SOC TOOL ANALYSIS:
Reveal Internal DNS, CDN, and Unknown Host.
Show traffic relationships.
Show two-way normal traffic where appropriate.

Stage 3 — CORRELATION:
Separate known-good activity from suspicious activity.
Show baseline-supported traffic differently from off-baseline traffic.

Stage 4 — ESCALATION:
Highlight suspicious infrastructure and evidence chain.
Show why the source is worth escalation.

Stage 5 — RESPONSE:
Show watchlist / monitoring update.
Do not show aggressive blocking in Assignment 000 unless already designed.
This is orientation, so consequence should be gentle.

Stage 6 — DEBRIEF:
Show final resolved learning map with labels:
known-good traffic
suspicious traffic
off-baseline source

198.51.100.20

Unknown external infrastructure requires validation. Analysts check ownership, ports touched, and whether it appears on the baseline.

## CONNECTION HOVER EXAMPLES

WS-4471 ↔ Internal DNS

Explanation:
Normal bidirectional traffic. Workstations commonly query DNS and receive responses.

WS-4471 ↔ CDN Service

Explanation:
Likely normal external traffic if the CDN is on the known-good baseline.

Unknown Host → WS-4471

Explanation:
One-way or repeated probing can indicate scanning. Analysts verify this by checking ports, ownership, and baseline.

Unknown Host → multiple ports

Explanation:
Touching several unrelated ports may suggest reconnaissance, especially if the host does not normally provide those services.
